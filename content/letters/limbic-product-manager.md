The Access brief reads like a description of how I already work: take a product that's past 0→1, owned by a small team carrying high load, and make the week-to-week cadence quieter and more predictable so senior product time can move to discovery elsewhere. The win here isn't a flashy roadmap. It's the engineering team trusting that shipping just happens, on rhythm, without anyone hovering.

I've run that rhythm under harder constraints. At Fold - personal finance powered by bitcoin, which went from ~11 people to the first Bitcoin-only company on the Nasdaq - I shipped against 2-engineer teams through constant pivots, in an environment where Legal, Banking, and Compliance all had veto power over what went out. The day-to-day was exactly the Access list: triage real issues from noise, route them to the right owner, hold the line on scope, and keep the next-highest-value project lined up. I shipped a [crypto-tax compliance integration](/case-studies/taxbit) (vendor pick through launch, six stakeholder groups, two engineers) 2.5 months ahead of deadline at 90% adoption, and rebuilt the [banking-partner approval process](/features/banking-partner-approval) so a 4-week regulatory gate dropped to days. Translating product into a regulated partner's language to unblock the roadmap is the same muscle your EHR and HL7/FHIR work will need.

When engineering had no bandwidth for a feature that had stalled for three years, I didn't wait for resources. I solo-built the [Notification Preference Center](/case-studies/notification-preference-center) (the in-app screen that lets users choose which alerts they get and on which channel) - design, backend, copy, launch. 50% of daily actives set a preference in the first month and the support tickets around notifications went quiet. Then I read the data to see who self-selected into which channel, and found that opt-outs actually raised per-channel engagement about 10% month over month, because the people who stayed on a channel were the ones who wanted it. The second-order insight mattered more than the launch.

That data habit is the part I'd lean on hardest for a healthcare front door. I pull and interrogate the numbers myself rather than asking for a dashboard and trusting it. On Fold's [rewards wheel](/case-studies/spin-wheel) - the gamified feature that handed out bitcoin and was both our most-loved feature and our biggest financial liability - I ran a live choice experiment that told us users actually preferred a known flat reward over the gamble, and I built the Power BI model myself to prove gift-card lift justified the reward spend and win Finance's sign-off. I re-tuned the wheel's economics to stay solvent at any bitcoin price up to $1M and turned a cost center into a six-figure sponsorship program. None of that happens if you can't read what the events are actually telling you, and where they'd mislead you.

On the AI-native point, the JD asks for someone who relies on AI heavily and goes past using it to write PRDs, without ever shipping slop. That's how I already build. I shipped a live multi-agent tool that runs my own job applications end to end - it researches the company, scores fit, and drafts every artifact through a set of specialized agents, with a mandatory human checkpoint before the expensive work runs and a verification pass on every file an agent writes. The lesson that built it is the one I'd bring to Access: AI is fast and confidently wrong often enough that you direct it with goals and stopping conditions, then verify before anything ships. That's the difference between leverage and slop.

Here's where I'd start. The immediate brief is to make the inbound quieter, so in week one I'd build the thing instead of describing it: a small triage console that ingests the mixed stream of bugs, debt, CS asks, and scope additions, classifies and routes each item with an AI pass, and renders one impact-scored queue so the next-highest-value project is always at the top - with the AI drafting the project overview for that top item and flagging scope-creep risk. It's a few hours of work for me, and it turns "separate real issues from noise" from a meeting into a tool the whole team can read.

The honest gap is healthcare. I've got no EHR or FHIR reps yet. The JD calls those a bonus rather than a requirement, and I'd close them the way I became Fold's crypto-tax subject-matter expert when no one else would touch it - by going deep fast because the roadmap was blocked on someone understanding it. The regulated, veto-heavy, get-it-wrong-and-it-matters shape of the work is one I've lived for four years.

I want the seat where I make a small team's shipping predictable, on a product that's already talking to millions of lives. That's the work I do best, and it's the work you're describing. I'd love to walk through how I'd sequence the first few cycles against your enterprise pipeline.
