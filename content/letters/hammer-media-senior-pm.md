## Notes & vision

What follows is the longer version of the work behind the letter - the specific observations I gathered from time in the app, cross-referenced against your public reviews and the version history. Read it as a punch list of things I'd want to dig into, not a list of recommendations I'm prescribing. The point is to show that I've done the work, not to grade yours.

### A few things I noticed up front

You rebranded from Simpler Budget to Dollarwise in version 4.0.0 about ten months ago. Then in 6.0.0, two months ago, you shipped a genuine product redesign - Safe to Spend, Swipe to Review, the 50/30/20 Display, the 12-category simplification, AI-Enhanced Insights. That's a lot of strategic motion for a team this small. It's also working in a way: the brand is right, the product is moving toward the brand, and you're already starting to lap competitors who haven't moved in years.

The places where I think the experience is still leaving value on the table fall into three buckets - trust, surface visibility, and the show-to-app loop.

### Trust - what users are saying and what I'd actually do about it

The most common review phrase since the redesign is some version of "the app doesn't work." Almost nobody from the team is replying. That's the cheapest week-one win on the board - free, zero engineering time, and star ratings move when users see a real human responding to feedback.

Below that surface trust problem there's a stack of specific issues I'd want to dig into. None of these need a process overhaul. Most are one or two days of focused work.

**Onboarding length.** I counted 18 screens from first download to getting into the app, then another 10+ through the bank-connect integration. The integration side you may not control. The 18 you do.

**The "redesign" trigger.** When a user without a connected bank opens the app, they hit the "Fresh New Look" / "Simpler Budget is now DollarWise" announcement screens. Useful once. Now those screens are showing up to brand-new users who don't know what the old brand was. Easy trigger fix.

**The 3-day trial.** Gating the app behind payment info before users have seen any value is the opposite of what I'd recommend. I rebuilt Fold's onboarding around the inverse move: users in immediately, 30-day trial with no payment required, feature-level paywalls instead of gating the whole experience. That doesn't mean my way is right for Dollarwise - I'd want to understand what the current model is solving for first. But it's the conversation I'd want to have early.

**Bank-connect provider.** The flow tries Mastercard Connect (via Finicity) first, then falls back to Plaid when Mastercard fails. My Wells Fargo failed on Mastercard, then succeeded on Plaid (after a silent first-try failure on Plaid too - no error, just didn't connect; second attempt worked). I'd want to see conversion data side-by-side. My strong prior is Plaid wins on the major banks. If true, lead with Plaid. Maintaining two integrations is probably costing more than one and adding support surface area.

**Duplicate transactions.** I'm getting duplicate transactions from a single connected account. If the data isn't trustworthy the app actively makes my life worse. That's a product-trust issue, not just a bug. Questions I'd want to answer week one: how is the app pulling data (cadence, deltas vs full snapshots)? Is categorization MCC-driven, vendor-name-driven, or both? What's the dedupe logic (per-transaction hash? per date+amount+counterparty)? How are ACH transfers categorized? How are credit-card payments from one linked account to another handled? (Rocket Money had this exact problem and it confused me as a user.)

**App Store review reply program.** Set up a process where every new review gets a real human reply within 48 hours. Free, no engineering, immediate trust signal.

### Surface visibility - the app's value compounds with each connected account

"Connect another account" lives in the profile tab. It should be a first-class entry point on the home page. The app's value compounds with each connected account - getting users to a full financial picture should be the second action after first connection, not something they have to hunt for.

Same goes for the manual-entry path. Users at neobanks not on Plaid or Finicity need a clearly visible "connect via account + routing" option on the institution-select page. Without it, those users just bail.

### Navigation - the 6-tap category change

To change a transaction category from the Insights view:

1. Tap Housing → see all txs in housing as little boxes
2. Tap a transaction box → redirected to the Activity tab with the tx prefilled in the search bar
3. See the highlighted transaction
4. Tap it again → opens the transaction detail page
5. Change category there

Six taps to change one category. There's no back button, so each tap loses context. The fix is press-and-hold on the tx tile from Insights and change category inline. That's a one-week design + eng effort that'd save hundreds of millions of taps over the user base.

### The marketing site

A few things worth flagging that aren't in the app itself:

**Cross-device CTA bug.** The marketing site shows iOS download buttons to desktop visitors. Click it on a Mac, you get the App Store with no install path. Probably the single highest-bail-rate bug on the site. Fix: device-detect on page load, show a QR code on desktop so users can scan to install on their phone. One-day engineering job, probably a measurable conversion lift.

**Value props.** The current marketing copy reads as retention-language for the existing audience ("we refreshed and redesigned"). New visitors don't know what was there before. Rewrite from a cold-audience POV.

**CTA placement.** Only at the top of the page. Anyone scrolling past the fold without converting has to scroll back to find a download button. Sticky CTA on mobile, repeated CTAs on desktop.

**No interactive demo.** The Swipe to Review flow would translate beautifully to an embedded marketing-site animation. Show, don't tell.

### Show-to-app loop - the gap I'm most curious about

This is the one strategic question I'd want to ask leadership before recommending anything: what's the explicit thinking on Caleb's in-product presence?

From the outside, the show and the app feel like two separate companies. Caleb's brand is the moat. The audience is the funnel. But there's no promo-code field in the payment flow (Caleb has given out codes in videos; users have nowhere to redeem them). No welcome state for users who came in from a YouTube link. No in-app section that ties back to the show. The annual plan bundles his meal-plan book - that's the only place Caleb shows up in-product.

There's a strategic call behind that separation. Maybe you want Dollarwise to stand on its own as a fintech, not as creator merch. That's defensible. I'd want to understand the thinking before assuming a direction. But if the call is "we just haven't gotten to it yet," there's a lot of compounding value sitting unclaimed.

Some moves worth scoping if the strategic posture allows:

- **Promo code field** in the payment flow + at signup. Lets Caleb's video codes actually work.
- **Welcome state** for users referred from YouTube. Acknowledges the path.
- **In-app section** for show content - latest episode, "tools used in this episode," etc.
- **Episode-tied features.** Tuesday episode is about a person with $80k of card debt → in-app debt-payoff prompt with an "as seen in this week's episode" badge.
- **Comments → product pipeline.** Someone reads YouTube comments weekly, patterns flow into product. Free research panel of 3M people.

### Brand and market position

You crack top 100 in finance subcategories but don't compete with household names yet. Your differentiator (simplified, beginner-friendly 50/30/20 budgeting) is real and underexploited. Apps with weaker brands and worse UX are still outranking you in the subcategories you should own.

The brand is right. The market doesn't fully feel it yet. The pivot to "simplified, beginner-friendly" is the smart move and 6.0.0 is real execution on that thesis. The work now is iterating with user research and signal data until the market feels it.

Fold went through several rebrands and product pivots before one stuck. The winning move was always taking the calculated risk and iterating. You've taken the risk. The job is iterating until it lands.

### User research - what I'd stand up in the first 60 days

A research operation that fits the team size, not a BigCo function:

- **Recent-signup interviews.** Users who signed up in the last 7 days. What got them to install. What almost made them bail. Whether the show was the path in.
- **Longtime-user interviews.** Power users on each plan tier. What features they actually use. What they'd hate to lose. What's missing.
- **Live observational studies.** Watch users do common tasks - connect account, set budget, categorize, review - with think-aloud protocol. The 6-tap category-change problem stops being theoretical the moment you see someone do it.
- **Surveys.** Two surveys split by tenure (new vs veteran) and behavior (active vs dormant, paid vs trial). Different segments give different signals.
- **Free month** for participants. Cheap recruitment for the audience that matters most.
- **Recruit through Caleb.** A community post or a short mention in an episode. The literal Friends of Fold playbook adapted for Hammer's audience.

### The strategic frame - where the category is going

The future of budgeting apps is being won quickly. The winners won't be the apps that track purchases best. That's table stakes now. The winners will be the apps that act on the user's behalf.

Once Dollarwise has the basics fixed, Caleb's audience becomes a cross-pollination funnel competitors would pay tens of millions for. The most obvious first move for a second product is a debt-payoff agent: auto-routes discretionary cash into the user's highest-interest debt line the day a paycheck lands. Caleb's audience is literally people drowning in debt. That product is the version of his show that lives in their wallet.

Other directions worth scoping eventually:

- **Savings layer** with automated sinking funds.
- **AI financial copilot** with natural-language commands ("can I afford a $1,200 vacation in July without dipping into emergency?").
- **Predictive cash-flow modeling** - dynamic purchase-impact previews, scenario stress-testing.
- **Co-managed budgeting for couples** with selective merge controls.

The debt-payoff agent is the right first move because it's on-brand, on-thesis, and the audience is literally pre-qualified by watching the show.

I succeed by delivering wins myself, picking battles, helping the team take more small steps instead of fewer big ones, and shipping fast.

The team has a way it does things. My job is to make those ways scale and make money while they do.
