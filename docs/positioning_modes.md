# David Steinbroner — Positioning Modes
*Living reference. Use this to pull the right framing before drafting any cover letter, blurb, or application material.*
*Last updated: February 2026*

---

## How to Use This Document

Each mode below is a distinct lens for presenting David's experience. The underlying facts don't change — what changes is which story is front and center, which proof points lead, and what language is used. Before drafting anything, identify which 1-2 modes map most directly to the role, then pull from those sections.

These modes are not mutually exclusive. Many cover letters blend 2-3. But always pick a lead mode and let the others support it — don't try to be everything at once.

### When a New JD Is Shared

**Always follow this sequence before drafting anything:**

1. Read the JD and identify the top 3-5 things the role actually needs (read between the lines, not just the bullet points)
2. Open this document and match those needs to modes
3. Recommend: which mode leads, which mode(s) support, and whether any proof from the Many-Hats layer should be woven in
4. Flag if the role suggests a new mode that doesn't exist yet
5. Only then proceed to drafting

The mode recommendation should come before any cover letter draft — every time, no exceptions.

## Mode 1: Compliance / Risk / Legal PM

**When to use:** Roles at regulated companies (fintech, insurtech, legaltech, payments, healthtech) where the job description mentions compliance, risk, legal coordination, regulatory environment, or trust & safety.

**The core identity:**
David is a PM who built systems to make regulated environments move faster — not by working around compliance, but by making compliance a product competency. He understands that in regulated industries, the person who can translate product into compliance language (and back) is the person who unblocks the roadmap.

**The narrative:**
At Fold, every feature David shipped had to pass through Legal, Banking Partners, Compliance, and often external counsel — all of whom had veto power. Rather than treating this as a bottleneck, David redesigned the system. He mapped the entire app, established direct relationships with banking partner compliance teams (bypassing slow account management layers), created forward-looking processes so partners saw what was coming before it shipped, and built internal compliance guides so teams stopped designing out-of-compliance features from the start. Approval timelines dropped 75%. Surprise scrambles stopped. The roadmap unlocked.

That same muscle showed up in the TaxBit implementation — but the TaxBit story is not the compliance story in full. It's one proof point within a larger pattern: David coordinated 6 stakeholder groups (Fold Legal, Compliance, Finance, external counsel, banking partner, card issuing platform, and the vendor itself) with 2 engineers and shipped 2.5 months early with 90% user adoption. He became the SME on crypto tax law because no one else would, then wrote all the user documentation and trained support.

**When to lead with the banking partner process vs. TaxBit:**
- **Lead with banking partner process** when the role is about regulatory navigation, systems-building, or ongoing compliance infrastructure — the process rebuild is the bigger, more sustained story
- **Lead with TaxBit** when the role signals vendor coordination, speed-to-ship under constraint, or cross-functional stakeholder management with hard deadlines — TaxBit shows David can coordinate complex multi-party projects and ship early with high adoption
- Both stories can appear in the same letter; the question is which earns the lead paragraph

**What this demonstrates:** Compliance is not a background detail of David's work. It was the primary terrain he operated in for four years.

**Lead proof points:**
- Reduced banking partner approval timelines from 4+ weeks to days (75% reduction)
- 6-party stakeholder coordination on TaxBit with 2 engineers — shipped 2.5 months early
- Built internal compliance guide that stopped teams from designing out-of-compliance features
- Rebuilt the entire approval system by establishing direct relationships with banking partner compliance contacts
- Shipped crypto tax compliance 2.5 months early, 90% adoption, 2% support ticket rate

**Key phrases / framing:**
- "Compliance as a product competency, not a bottleneck"
- "The PM who owned the translation layer between product and regulated partners"
- "Built systems that made compliance a competitive advantage"
- "Every feature I shipped touched payments, compliance, and risk — where one wrong move meant real financial exposure"
- "Legal, Banking, and Engineering all had veto power. I was the person who got it through anyway."

**Avoid:**
- Leading with TaxBit as the first or only example — it's one proof point in a larger compliance story
- Framing this as "I worked with compliance" — the framing is "I rebuilt how compliance worked"

## Mode 2: Fintech PM (Broad)

**When to use:** Fintech roles that aren't specifically crypto or compliance-focused — payments, consumer financial products, rewards, card products, banking infrastructure, BaaS, lending.

It was a Bitcoin-native financial platform where every dollar a user touched could earn rewards, move freely between fiat and Bitcoin, and stay entirely inside the ecosystem.

David helped lead the rebrand: understanding that the ACH infrastructure gave Fold real financial credibility, developing rewards programs for it, and communicating its value to users in a way that changed how they thought about the product. That's the PM move of seeing the asset you already have and naming it correctly.

The Flywheel features required coordinating every stakeholder simultaneously: Engineering, Finance, Compliance, Support, Design, Marketing, the CEO internally — and custodians, banking partners, Visa, and external counsel externally. Shipping all three while holding that web together is what earned Fold its seat at the table with Cash App and Coinbase.

He also managed one of the hardest economics problems in consumer fintech: a rewards feature (the Spin Wheel) that users loved but that was financially unsustainable. He fixed it — 4 iterations, 18 months, turned it into a model that worked at any Bitcoin price and generated six-figure sponsorship revenue from a former cost center.

- Spin Wheel: Turned unsustainable prize economics into a model that works at any Bitcoin price up to $1M; six-figure sponsorship revenue from a former cost center
- Banking partner infrastructure: Rebuilt the system that made fintech partnerships actually work — 75% faster approvals

## The Many-Hats Layer
*This is not a mode. It's a proof layer — supporting evidence that can be woven into any mode when the role rewards scrappy execution, lean-team experience, or range across functions. Use it when a title-only read of David's resume might underestimate the scope of what he was actually doing.*

**The context:**
David's title progression at Fold — Community Manager → Growth Manager → Product Marketing Manager → Product Manager → Product Operations and Strategy Specialist — makes it look like a linear climb. It wasn't. For most of his time at Fold, he was doing multiple of these jobs simultaneously, with no dedicated budget, often no engineering bandwidth, and no design support. He wasn't handed a function. He built one, then another, then ran them in parallel while shipping product.

And this pattern didn't start at Fold. At Mosaic, David was in a customer support role when he started building the company's process documentation from scratch — a function that didn't exist. He identified the need, owned it, and opened a second call center because of the system he built. At Sensor Tower, he was a Customer Success Specialist who was also tracking adoption patterns and feeding them back into product priorities — doing informal product research without the title. The many-hats tendency is not a Fold artifact. It's how David has always operated.

**What "many hats" actually looked like:**
- Community Manager + Growth Manager at the same time: Rebuilding a dormant Discord into a real 45K-member community while running growth experiments, attribution, ASO, and Flash Stacks — all simultaneously, all without a team
- Community + Growth + Product Marketing simultaneously: Owned all launches and GTM while managing the Spin Wheel and running the community. Three functions, one person.
- PM with no engineering bandwidth: Solo-shipped the Notification Preference Center (design, backend integration, copy, launch) because engineering wasn't available. That's not a gap — that's range.
- PM + internal systems builder: Built the banking partner approval process, the compliance guides, the Spin Wheel admin tooling, and the Zapier/Zendesk support pipeline — none of which were assigned to him, all of which made the company work better.
- PM + vendor coordinator + legal partner + customer-facing negotiator: Credit card work was two people (David + GM of Credit) doing the job of an entire team at a larger company.

**The framing:**
This isn't "I wore many hats." That's a cliché. The framing is: David operated in an environment where the only way to survive was to identify what the company needed, own it, and ship it — regardless of title, bandwidth, or resources. That's not generalism. That's the specific skill of knowing what matters most and finding a way to do it.

**When to use this layer:**
- Startup or Series A-B roles where "scrappy" and "takes initiative" are explicit signals
- Roles where the JD mentions "cross-functional" but really means "you'll do multiple things"
- When bridging a gap (e.g., "we want a growth PM" and David's growth work was done under a different title)
- When the hiring manager might underread David's scope based on title alone
- Any time "bandwidth constraints" or "lean team" are mentioned

**Key phrases / framing:**
- "I didn't wait for the function to exist. I built it."
- "At a 15-person startup competing against Cash App, there's no room for 'that's not my job.'"
- "Startup-to-Nasdaq means you do the work that needs to be done, not the work on your job description."
